In Scotland, play is recognised as a vital part of a happy, healthy childhood. It is not an "add-on" to the school day, but a fundamental right and a core pillar of the **Curriculum for Excellence**.

## The Play Strategy for Scotland

The Scottish Government’s **Play Strategy for Scotland** sets out a vision where all children have "access to high-quality play opportunities" every day. The strategy identifies schools as primary locations for this development.

Key principles from the strategy that drive our project include:

- **Play as a Right:** Every child has the right to play under **Article 31 of the UNCRC**.
- **The Power of Outdoors:** The strategy prioritises outdoor play as it provides the most diverse and challenging environment for children to grow.
- **Intrinsic Value:** Play is essential for its own sake—it is how children make sense of the world, build friendships, and develop resilience.

## The Challenge: Falling Short of Modern Standards

While the commitment of our staff and families is high, we must recognise that the **current physical environment at Viewlands unfortunately falls short** of the standards set by modern Scottish educational policy.

### 1. The "Asphalt Gap"

The **Play Strategy for Scotland** emphasises the need for "rich, stimulating environments." Currently, our playground is largely a **sparse and exposed** asphalt-dominated space. Research shows that uniform surfaces:

- Limit the range of physical movements children perform.
- Lead to "football dominance," where high-energy games unintentionally exclude children seeking quieter or more creative play.

### 2. Barriers to Year-Round Play

The **Curriculum for Excellence** expects outdoor learning and play to happen in all weathers. However, our "top of the hill" location and lack of effective shelter currently mean that during wet or windy periods, the educational benefits of the outdoors are severely diminished.

### 3. Lack of Managed Risk

Modern education standards (as highlighted by **Play Scotland**) advocate for "challenging play." A sparse playground offers very few opportunities for children to experience **managed risk**, which is essential for building confidence and lifelong resilience.
